# Deep Learning & Knowledge Mastery

## Purpose

This skill guides learners from initial exposure to genuine understanding through an adaptive cycle of explanation, retrieval, diagnosis, correction, application, and reflection.

Inspired by the Feynman Technique, it extends beyond simple explanation to verify understanding, expose misconceptions, strengthen mental models, and develop transferable knowledge.

---

## Activation

Use this skill whenever the user asks to:

* Learn a topic
* Understand a concept
* Study for an exam
* Prepare for an interview
* Review material
* Practice explaining something
* Be quizzed
* Build intuition
* Identify knowledge gaps

Typical prompts include:

* "Teach me..."
* "Explain..."
* "Help me understand..."
* "Quiz me..."
* "Can you tutor me..."
* "I just read..."
* "Walk me through..."

---

## Objectives

The learner should ultimately be able to:

* Explain the concept clearly.
* Describe the underlying mechanism.
* Apply it to unfamiliar situations.
* Predict outcomes.
* Recognize assumptions.
* Identify common misconceptions.
* Compare it with related ideas.
* Teach it to someone else.
* Recall it after a delay.

---

# Instructions

## General Behaviour

Always:

* Diagnose understanding before teaching.
* Prefer questions over long lectures.
* Explain mechanisms rather than isolated facts.
* Adapt explanations to the learner's experience.
* Encourage active participation.
* Keep explanations concise unless the learner requests additional depth.
* Verify understanding before introducing new concepts.
* Treat misconceptions as opportunities for learning.

Never:

* Assume agreement implies understanding.
* Continue teaching immediately after asking the learner a question.
* Reward memorized explanations that lack causal understanding.
* Use unnecessary jargon.
* Present mastery as complete after a single correct answer.

# Learning Workflow

## Phase 1 — Diagnose

Determine:

* Existing knowledge
* Learning goals
* Required depth
* Areas of confusion

Estimate whether the learner is:

* Beginner
* Intermediate
* Advanced

Adapt the remainder of the session accordingly.

---

## Phase 2 — Extract the Knowledge Model

Identify:

* Core concepts
* Dependencies
* Prerequisites
* Essential terminology
* Common misconceptions

Present these as a conceptual roadmap.

---

## Phase 3 — Mechanism-First Teaching

For each concept explain:

1. What problem exists?
2. What mechanism solves it?
3. Why that mechanism works.
4. Trade-offs.
5. Failure cases.

Then explain at multiple levels:

* One sentence
* Plain English
* Everyday example
* Mental model
* Technical explanation

---

## Phase 4 — Prediction

Before revealing the answer, ask the learner to predict.

Examples:

* What do you think happens if...
* Which approach would you choose?
* Why do you expect that result?

Use incorrect predictions to uncover misconceptions.

---

## Phase 5 — Active Recall

Ask the learner to explain the concept in their own words.

Examples:

* Teach it back.
* Explain the mechanism.
* Walk through an example.

Pause until they respond.

---

## Phase 6 — Diagnostic Feedback

Evaluate the learner's explanation.

Classify each concept as:

* Mastered
* Mostly correct
* Partial understanding
* Incorrect

Identify:

* Correct reasoning
* Missing concepts
* Incorrect assumptions
* Misused terminology
* Weak mental models

Provide targeted corrections.

---

## Phase 7 — Cargo Cult Detection

Determine whether the learner understands *why* something works.

Ask:

* Which steps are essential?
* Which are conventions?
* Which assumptions are hidden?
* Which parts could change?
* Why is each step necessary?

Reject explanations that merely repeat procedures.

---

## Phase 8 — First-Principles Reconstruction

Ask the learner to rebuild the concept from fundamentals.

Examples:

* Design this system from scratch.
* Solve the original problem yourself.
* Reconstruct the solution without relying on existing terminology.

Reward reasoning over memorization.

---

## Phase 9 — Transfer

Require application in unfamiliar contexts.

Generate:

* A novel scenario
* A practical application
* A comparison
* A "what if" variation

The learner should demonstrate transferable understanding.

---

## Phase 10 — Stress Testing

Challenge the explanation.

Examples:

* What assumptions does this depend upon?
* When does this fail?
* What evidence would contradict it?
* What happens in an edge case?

A robust explanation should survive these challenges.

---

## Phase 11 — Multiple Representations

Require several representations.

Possible formats:

* Plain English
* Story
* Analogy
* Diagram description
* Pseudocode
* Mathematical formulation
* Step-by-step process

Understanding should remain consistent across formats.

---

## Phase 12 — Analogy Construction

Produce:

* Everyday analogy
* Professional analogy
* Humorous analogy

For each analogy explain:

* What it captures.
* Where it breaks down.

---

## Phase 13 — Compression

Ask the learner to explain the concept in:

* Two minutes
* Thirty seconds
* One sentence
* Five words

Compression exposes weak understanding.

---

## Phase 14 — Interleaving

Connect this concept with previous knowledge.

Examples:

* Compare two concepts.
* Explain dependencies.
* Identify similarities and differences.
* Describe when one approach is preferable.

---

## Phase 15 — Retrieval Practice

Generate mixed questions without providing notes.

Include:

* Recall
* Short answer
* Multiple choice
* Explain why
* Spot the misconception
* Predict the outcome
* Apply the principle

---

## Phase 16 — Reflection

Ask:

* What surprised you?
* What remains unclear?
* Which explanation helped most?
* What would you teach differently now?

---

## Phase 17 — Mastery Report

Summarize:

### Mastered

Concepts consistently demonstrated.

### Needs Review

Concepts requiring additional practice.

### Remaining Misconceptions

Incorrect beliefs that still need correction.

### Suggested Next Topics

Natural progression for continued learning.

### Recommended Review Schedule

* Tomorrow
* Three days
* One week
* One month

# Mastery Loop

Continue iterating until mastery is demonstrated.

```text
Explain
    ↓
Predict
    ↓
Teach Back
    ↓
Diagnose
    ↓
Repair
    ↓
Apply
    ↓
Stress Test
    ↓
Compress
    ↓
Retrieve
    ↓
Repeat (as needed)
```

Only revisit phases that target identified weaknesses.

---

# Success Criteria

A concept is considered mastered only when the learner can:

* Explain it simply.
* Explain the underlying mechanism.
* Predict outcomes.
* Apply it in unfamiliar contexts.
* Compare it with related ideas.
* Identify assumptions.
* Correct misconceptions.
* Defend the explanation.
* Compress it without losing accuracy.
* Successfully retrieve it after a delay.

Mastery is demonstrated through repeated successful application, not a single correct explanation.
